Universal Nuclear Scaling as a Mixture of Core‑Compression Laws

Tracy McSheery

Abstract

We extend the two-term charge–mass scaling law, Q(A) = c₁ A^(2/3) + c₂ A, to a finite mixture of components. Across all known nuclides, a single global curve achieves RMSE = 3.8242 and R² = 0.979376. Allowing three components and assigning each nuclide to its most probable line reduces error significantly: K=3 (hard bins) yields RMSE = 1.4559 and R² = 0.997011; responsibility-weighted predictions improve further to RMSE = 1.1069 and R² = 0.998272. These results indicate that three compact two-term baselines capture nearly all residual structure, with shell effects persisting as small perturbations within each track.

Introduction

A two-term charge–mass law, Q(A) = c₁ A^(2/3) + c₂ A, captures nearly all nuclides with high fidelity. We generalize to a finite mixture of such laws and show that K=3 components provide a superior description, revealing three tracks that map naturally to nucleosynthetic regimes.

Methods

We model Q as a linear function of [A^(2/3), A] with no intercept. We fit a Gaussian mixture-of-regressions via Expectation–Maximization: responsibilities are updated from component residuals, and component parameters (c₁, c₂, σ², π) are obtained by weighted least squares and moment updates. Model order K is assessed by BIC and K-fold cross-validated test log-likelihood.

Model selection

Model selection (all nuclides):
• K=1: log L = -16125.6,  BIC = 32277,  AIC = 32257
• K=2: log L = -15833.8,  BIC = 31728,  AIC = 31682
• K=3: log L = -15736.0,  BIC = 31567,  AIC = 31494

Cross-validation (3-fold): average Δ test log-likelihood (K=3 − K=1) = 126.8, favoring K=3.

Discussion

A small number of nucleosynthetic regimes appear to imprint distinct charge–mass baselines across both stable and unstable nuclides. This provides a parsimonious layer between global scaling and shell-model corrections. Future work will couple the mixture components to astrophysical labels (e.g., r-/s-/rp-/p-process–dominant sets), quantify per-component residuals near magic numbers, and explore physics-informed priors for (c₁, c₂).

Conclusion

A universal two-term baseline is sufficient to organize the nuclide chart, and a finite mixture of three such baselines substantially improves fit quality—cutting RMSE by roughly 2.6–3.5× relative to a single curve and raising R² to ~0.997–0.998. The three lines align with neutron‑rich, valley‑like, and proton‑rich tracks, consistent with multiple nucleosynthetic regimes. Future work will associate components with astrophysical labels and quantify per‑component residuals near magic numbers.
